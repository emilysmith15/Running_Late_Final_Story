Companies Hired by the MTA to Transport New Yorkers with Disabilities are Running Late. But the MTA Keeps Shelling Out

Since late 2024, rides for New Yorkers with disabilities provided by these companies are taking more than double the time promised at an increasing rate.

By Emily Smith
May 20, 2026

Kate Interrante, 58, gets out of work at 5 p.m. every day, from the Barclay–Vesey Building downtown. Sometimes, she doesn’t get home to Glendale, Queens until 8:30 p.m.

Interrante relies on an MTA program called Access-a-Ride because she has an autoimmune disease that often prevents her from walking, and a heart condition that causes heart episodes. Access-a-Ride was implemented in 1990 to provide door-to-door transportation for New Yorkers with disabilities.

Because she has a subscription with the service, a vehicle is supposed to pick her up every weekday, for the same price that it costs to take the subway. The problem is, they often don’t come at 5 p.m., when they’re scheduled to arrive.

“It got to the point where me and another coworker bring a deck,” she said. “We'll play several hands of Uno while waiting.”

A potential explanation for the delays is that the cars being sent to pick her up are driven by brokered car companies, hired by the MTA to bring Access-a-Ride users around the city. In addition to the Access-a-Ride vehicles operated by the MTA, the authority contracts broker transportation companies to drive additional riders.

These companies have faced increasing scrutiny since the program began in 2011. Now, according to MTA data, rides provided by these brokers make up a majority of trips taken by users – they’re so common that riders know the companies that drive them by name. Interrante said that 90% of the time her car is operated by the Corporate Transportation Group, known as CTG by users.

In 2025, the MTA paid more than $185 million to the Corporate Transportation Group to provide broker car services for Access-a-Ride, according to MTA data. The MTA spent the most on new train prototypes and a major station repair on Park Avenue that year, but the contract with the Corporate Transportation Group accounted for the next biggest share of MTA spending. For every year since 2023, payouts to the Corporate Transportation Group have been in the top four most expensive for the MTA.

But, according to users, the increase in budget hasn’t meant better service. Tracy Dwyer, 65, said she uses Access-a-Ride to get from her home in Staten Island to work in Lower Manhattan. She has used the service since 2013, after she lost her ability to walk without assistance.

She has a subscription that means she should be dropped off at work by 8:30 a.m., every weekday. Often, multiple different broker cars are assigned to pick her up before one finally shows up, sometimes hours late.

Because of this, she said she got a note from her doctor that requires Access-a-Ride to only assign her traditional Access-a-Rides, or large, blue and white vans driven and operated by the MTA. She said sometimes, she still gets assigned broker cars. When she tried to schedule plans for Mother’s Day, she was assigned a broker car.

“I wound up canceling my whole plans,” she said. “I can't get stranded.”

Since last April, the percentage of broker company-operated trips that took more than double the time they were supposed to take has grown more than ten-fold, according to MTA data. In April 2025, this only happened 6% of the time, but in April 2026, it was happening more than 85% of the time.

The MTA started using broker car companies to provide rides with the intention of expanding paratransit services to more riders while saving on costs. Since October 2016, the earliest available data, the total number of trips taken per month has more than doubled. Access-a-Ride hit its first million-trip-month in October 2025, and March and April 2026 became the second and third.

“The system of using brokered vehicles has expanded Access-A-Ride a lot,” said Evan Yankey, an advocacy director for the Brooklyn Center for Independence of the Disabled. “And that is good.”

He said that expanding Access-a-Ride to more New Yorkers is essential for those with disabilities. In the last year, the program has expanded faster than ever before. Throughout 2025, the number of people with disabilities who are registered to use Access-a-Ride saw a huge spike, with a 13% increase. Between January 2022 and January 2025, registrants had only increased by 5%, over three years, according to MTA data. Now, over 200,000 New Yorkers are registered with Access-a-Ride.

Michael Ring, 62, a member of the MTA Advisory Committee for Transit Accessibility (ACTA), the vice president of the disability advocacy group Disabled in Action, and an Access-a-Ride user, said having door-to-door transportation allows him and other New Yorkers with disabilities to “function and contribute to society.”

“You can use it to go to work, to go to the movies, to go bar hopping, to go rob a bank,” he said.

“The fact that we have the option of Paratransit and Access-a-Ride is a great feature of New York City,” said Jeff Peters, the director of communications for the Center for Independence of the Disabled, New York. But, he said it’s important to make sure the service is properly functioning.

Peters said a common complaint he hears at the center is that riders are sent on long, winding drives that drop off multiple riders in different boroughs during the same trip.

“They're taking you on wild ass trips,” said Dwyer.

She said she usually gets picked up an hour or two in advance to get to work by 8:30 a.m., but still is often dropped off late. Sometimes the trip only takes 25 minutes, but sometimes, it takes three hours.

“There are times they take me to Brownsville, they take me to Canarsie, they take me to Coney Island,” she said.

Dwyer and Peters said this issue impacts both traditional, MTA-operated services and broker car services. But, according to data from the MTA, this problem impacts broker car services more. While broker company operated-trips are taking double the time they’re supposed to, most of the time, this is only happening to MTA-operated services around 1% of the time, and this has been the case for many years.

Peters said that as a result of Access-a-Ride’s reputation for sending riders on long, multi-borough trips, it has earned the nickname, “stress-a-ride.”

The MTA declined to comment on this story.

Methodology

There are multiple components to this story that required data analysis.

The first relates to Access-a-Ride ride time data, which evaluates GPS data collected by the MTA that shows the percentage difference between the actual length of the ride and the scheduled length. It can be found on NYS’s Open Data platform, called “MTA NYCT Paratransit Ride Time: Beginning 2017.” This data was cleaned in Excel and interrogated in Jupyter Notebook for what percentage of rides ended up taking over double the scheduled ride time. It is relatively unclear how the MTA determines scheduled and actual ride time, but the MTA declined to comment on the issue. It is clear that Access-a-Ride assigns a user both a pick-up and a drop-off time; users are able to choose one when scheduling their appointments, but not both. It’s unclear how the MTA determines the length of time between the scheduled pick-up and drop-off, or what is included in that time. It is unclear whether it accounts for the time spent dropping off other passengers, but multiple sources reported being late to scheduled drop-offs as a result of these instances. Therefore, it appears that they either do not account for these or they do so inaccurately. It is also worth noting that winter storms impact NYC transportation in January and February 2026, and inevitably impacted ride time data. However, issues relayed by this data analysis were already on the rise before the storms and have not yet recovered.

The second data analysis relates to MTA procurement data, which shows MTA spending on contracts with outside companies, and can be found on NYS’s Open Data platform, called “MTA Procurements: Beginning 2018.” This data was cleaned in Excel and then interrogated in Jupyter Notebook to determine what the most expensive contracts were for the MTA, by year, for 2021-2025, as well as how much the MTA has paid, separately, each year, to each of the four broker car service companies who had their contracts renewed in June 2024.
